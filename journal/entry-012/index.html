Intertemporal Optimization: Shifting from Scalar Wants to Vector Trajectories

Most resolutions fail because they are static destinations, not dynamic functions. We apply Hyperbolic Discounting and Vector Calculus to prove that long-term success requires directional consistency over intensity.

The Incident Report

Most “New Year’s Resolutions” are destined for systemic failure because they are treated as static destinations rather than dynamic functions. We say, “I want to be fit” (a state), rather than “I want to optimize my metabolic rate over time” (a process).

A recent entry from the archives highlights a crucial cognitive pivot required for high-functioning systems:

“Think in terms of trajectory… Human life is constrained by delayed gratification… No important feat is accomplished immediately and must be mapped throughout time and results.”

This insight bridges the gap between simple desire and engineering reality. In physics, there is a fundamental difference between speed and velocity. Speed is a scalar quantity—it measures how fast you are moving. Velocity is a vector quantity—it measures how fast you are moving and in what direction.

To “think in terms of trajectory” is to abandon the scalar pursuit of “doing more” and embrace the vector pursuit of directional consistency.

1. The Biology of “Now”: Understanding Hyperbolic Discounting

The Insight:

“Human life is constrained by delayed gratification.”

The Science:

Biologically, humans are not naturally wired for long-term trajectories; we are wired for immediate resource acquisition. This phenomenon is modeled in Behavioral Economics as Hyperbolic Discounting, a theory developed by psychologist George Ainslie [1].

The subjective value (V) of a future reward is calculated as:

V = A / (1 + kD)

Where:

  • A = The actual amount of the reward (e.g., a healthy body).
  • D = The delay until you receive the reward (e.g., 6 months).
  • k = A parameter representing your degree of impatience (impulsivity).

The Synthesis:

When you feel the urge to quit a long-term project for a short-term pleasure (like scrolling social media or skipping a workout), it is because the Delay (D) is large, crashing the Value (V) of your goal towards zero. Your brain is not “weak”; it is simply running an equation where k is too high. The goal of “delayed gratification” is not moral fortitude; it is the systemic reduction of variable k.

2. From Scalar to Vector: The Physics of Trajectory

The Insight:

“Think in terms of trajectory.”

The Science:

A trajectory is a path followed by an object moving under the action of given forces. In your life, this is a Vector.

If you work 80 hours a week (Magnitude) but change your strategy every month (Direction), your net displacement is zero. You have high speed (scalar) but zero velocity (vector).

Mathematically, a successful life is not a point on a graph; it is an Integral. You are looking for the area under the curve of your effort over time.

The Synthesis:

The journal notes that results must be “mapped throughout time.” This is the integration process. A massive burst of effort today (a high spike on the graph) is mathematically less valuable than a moderate, sustained effort over a long duration (a large area under the curve). Consistency is not just a virtue; it is a volume multiplier in the calculus of achievement.

3. The Lag Function: Why We Quit Too Early

The Insight:

“No important feat is accomplished immediately.”

The Science:

In Control Theory, systems often experience Hysteresis or lag. When you input energy into a system (e.g., studying a new skill), the output (competence) is not linear. It often follows a sigmoid curve or an exponential growth curve where the initial results are negligible [2].

The Synthesis:

Most people quit during the “Flatline of Latent Potential”—the period where Input exceeds Output. They perceive this as failure. However, an engineer views this as potential energy accumulation. You are building the necessary activation energy to bridge the gap between your current state and the phase shift (the breakthrough).

The Application: The Vector Alignment Protocol

Theory is useless without execution. To move from “resolutions” to “trajectories,” you must implement a system that accounts for the time-delay of results.

Phase 1: The Vector Audit (Diagnosis)

Analyze your current efforts to ensure they have both magnitude and direction.

  • List your top 3 recurring activities.
  • Assign a Direction: Do these point toward your 5-year objective, or are they orthogonal (sideways) distractions?
  • The Heuristic: If an action has high magnitude (takes a lot of effort) but unclear direction, it is “Scalar Waste.” Eliminate it.

Phase 2: Reduce Variable k (Intervention)

You cannot rely on willpower; you must manipulate the variables in the Hyperbolic Discounting formula.

  • Decrease D (Delay): Break the 5-year goal into 2-week “sprints.” If the reward is too far away, the brain assigns it zero value. Create intermediate checkpoints.
  • Increase A (Visible Reward): Visualize the data. Use a streak counter or a progress graph. Making the progress visible increases the immediate value of the work.

Phase 3: The Integration Loop (Implementation)

Stop measuring “Success” (which is a lagging indicator). Start measuring “Trajectory” (a leading indicator).

  • The Protocol: Every evening, do not ask “Did I succeed?” Ask “Did I maintain the vector?”
  • The Metric: Trajectory Ratio = Hours on Primary Objective / Total Waking Hours.
  • Goal: Push this ratio above 0.2 (20 percent of your day focused on the primary trajectory).

Conclusion

The journal entry correctly identifies that human constraints are temporal. We are biological machines stuck in the present, trying to optimize for a future we cannot touch.

By shifting your mental model from Scalar (intensity of the moment) to Vector (magnitude plus direction over time), you stop fighting against time and start leveraging it as a compounding variable.

“Amateurs focus on the position (where they are). Masters focus on the derivative (the rate and direction of their change).”
